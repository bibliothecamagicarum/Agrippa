## Overview

**Johann Weyer** (also **Johannes Weyer** or **John Weyer**, 1515–1588) was a Dutch physician and demonologist who studied under Agrippa from ages 14 to 18 (1530–1534/1535) — the final years of Agrippa's life, during which he published both *De incertitudine* (1527) and *De occulta philosophia* (1533). Weyer is best known for his *De praestigiis daemonum* (1563), one of the earliest works to question the reality of witchcraft, which he wrote as a direct response to the witch trial craze sweeping the Continent during the Council of Trent era.

Weyer represents the turning point in the witchcraft debate: he was trained in Agrippa's magic, accepted the reality of demonic interference in the world, but concluded that the witch trials were a mass delusion built on demonic deception and ecclesiastical credulity.

## Apprentice to Agrippa

Weyer's apprenticeship to Agrippa between 1530 and 1534/1535 is documented by Hanegraaff:

 > 
 > "From age fourteen to eighteen, between 1530 and 1534/1535, Weyer was an apprentice to Cornelius Agrippa. These were the final years of Agrippa's life, during which he published his two main works, *De incertitudine* and *De occulta philosophia libri tres*, so Weyer must have been well aware of how Agrippa combined his adherence to the ancient wisdom and the *artes magicae* with a 'skeptical fideism' very similar to Gianfrancesco Pico's."

\[Hanegraaff, *Esotericism and the Academy*, p. 162\]

Weyer was thus intimately aware of both halves of Agrippa's thought: the occult philosophy with its theurgical ambitions, and the skeptical fideism that subordinated all human reason to faith alone.

## Skeptical Fideism and the Limits of Magic

Weyer absorbed Agrippa's skeptical fideism but drew a different conclusion. Where Agrippa held that magic had limits but real power within those limits — the theurgic reconciliation with God — Weyer moved closer to Gianfrancesco Pico's position: the magical arts were not merely limited but **pernicious and dangerous**.

Agrippa's position, as Weyer's source knew:

 > 
 > "God's revelation in the Bible, Agrippa points out, cannot be grasped by any judgment of our senses, by any reasoning of our mind, by any syllogism delivering proof, by any science, by any speculation, by any contemplation, in short, by any human powers, but only by faith in Jesus Christ, poured into our soul by God the Father through the intermediary of the Holy Spirit . . . \[T\]he intellect . . . must be subjected to faith, and faith must not give way to the intellect."

\[Agrippa, *Opera*, 299 and 553; trans. van der Poel, *Cornelius Agrippa*, pp. 57–58 and 103; cited in Hanegraaff, p. 165\]

Weyer agreed that faith was paramount but concluded that magical practice — even when not diabolical — was spiritually dangerous and intellectually deceptive.

## De Praestigiis Daemonum (1563)

*De praestigiis daemonum* (*On the Delusions of Demons*) stands at the beginning of the period when the witch hunt was gaining momentum on the Continent. Weyer's argument:

1. Demons are real, but their primary method is **deception**, not material harm
1. Witches are predominantly elderly, poor women whose "confessions" are extracted under torture or induced by demonic hallucinations
1. The Inquisition's "elaborated theory of witchcraft" is a construction, not a revelation
1. Most accused witches are not engaged in maleficium at all — they are victims of demonic illusions confirmed by ecclesiastical credulity

Weyer defended his master Agrippa against accusations of black magic, confirming Agrippa's piety was genuine. But unlike Agrippa, he did not believe the theurgic path was salvifically valuable.

## Weyer's Demonological Genealogy

Where Agrippa presented theurgic magic as a path to God, Weyer inverted the genealogy. In *De praestigiis daemonum*, the lineage of magic becomes explicitly demonic:

1. Fallen angels brought the knowledge of magic to earth
1. This was transmitted through Ham's son Misraim (= Zoroaster)
1. The Egyptians, Babylonians, and Persians transmitted it further
1. Pythagoras, Democritus, and Plato picked it up during travels
1. The lineage continues through the neoplatonists

Critically, Hanegraaff notes that Weyer's demonic genealogy **does not include the Florentine Platonists** (Ficino, Pico, Agrippa) — Weyer's own formation as Agrippa's student shielded the Hermetic tradition from the genealogical stigmatization it received in later demonological literature.

## Relationship to the Witch Craze

Weyer observed that the witch craze intensified precisely when the Church began destroying the "natural blending of paganism and Christianity" that had sustained medieval peasant culture. The witch trials were not a correction of pagan error but its tragic misidentification:

 > 
 > "That natural blending of paganism and Christianity – which had sustained the masses of peasants confronted with all sorts of dangers, real or imaginary – was being destroyed by the actions of the renewed Church."

\[Mora, "Introduction" to *Witches, Devils, and Doctors*; cited in Hanegraaff, p. 166\]

## Influence

Weyer's work began the medicalization and rationalization of witchcraft skepticism. He did not deny demons or magic — he simply argued that most accused witches were not real witches. This position was later developed by Reginald Scot (*The Discoverie of Witchcraft*, 1584) and others, and ultimately influenced the decline of secular witch prosecutions in the 17th century.
