Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa von Nettesheim

Overview

Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa von Nettesheim (1486–1535) was a German Renaissance humanist, physician, soldier, theologian, and occult philosopher. Author of De occulta philosophia libri tres (1533), a major summa of Renaissance learned magic. His intellectual trajectory spans early enthusiasm for Hermetic magic through to a complex mature position that harmonizes faith and reason.

Key Facts

  • Born: 1486, Nettesheim (near Cologne)
  • Died: 1535, Grenoble (imprisoned, died under uncertain circumstances)
  • Education: University of Cologne; studied law at Avignon, Pavia, Turin
  • Key period: Italian period (1509–1518) — formative years for his Hermetic interests

Major Works

  • De occulta philosophia libri tres (1533) — final version; three books on natural, celestial, and ceremonial magic
  • De occulta philosophia (first draft, 1510) — original manuscript sent to Trithemius
  • De incertitudine et vanitate scientiarum (1527, pub. 1530) — De vanitate; sceptical declamation against sciences
  • De triplici ratione cognoscendi Deum (1516) — three ways of knowing God
  • De originali peccato (1518) — on original sin
  • Oratio habita Papiae (1515) — university lecture on Hermes Trismegistus
  • Dialogus de Homine (1516) — dialogue on the nature of man

Key Doctrines

Threefold Magic

Agrippa divides magic into three levels: (1) physical/sublunar, (2) celestial/mathematical, (3) religious/ceremonial theurgy. Magia is the umbrella term for all three; the true magic is the divine theurgy of level three.

Divine Generation (Lazzarellian)

The apex of magic is not procedural technique but spiritual regeneration. The soul reborn from God participates in God's creative power — even the power to create souls. This is the arcanum revealed by Christ, accessible in this life.

Faith and Reason Harmony

The De vanitate attacks scholastic reason divorced from faith, not magic itself. Scientific legitimacy requires orientation toward God. Alchemy is valid when practiced by the spiritually regenerated.

Dispersa Intentio

Agrippa deliberately conceals his central doctrines in scattered, fragmentary passages across De occulta philosophia, requiring the reader to assemble the "dispersed intention." This follows an alchemical literary tradition.

Historiographical Significance

Agrippa has been read as: (a) a sincere magician seeking to restore ancient wisdom, (b) a sceptical ironist who exposed the vanity of all occult sciences, (c) a deliberate esoteric writer concealing heterodox Christianity behind a magical facade. Hanegraaff and Perrone Compagni both argue for the third reading — but through different emphases (Lazzarellian gnosis vs. alchemical integration).

Skeptical Turn and De Incertitudine

After 1527, Agrippa published De incertitudine et vanitate scientiarum (written 1527, published 1530), a radical skeptical declamation attacking all human knowledge. Sledge argues this is not a genuine repudiation of magic but a complex philosophical position:

  • The De vanitate attacks scholastic reason divorced from faith, not magic itself
  • Agrippa's skepticism directly influenced Descartes' method of doubt — the rationalist turn was inspired by reading Agrippa
  • This creates a tension: Occult Philosophy (magic) vs. De vanitate (radical skepticism and fideism) — Sledge calls this the central interpretive puzzle of Agrippa's thought
  • The tension may be resolved by understanding Agrippa as an esoteric writer: he reveals truth only to the initiated; the De vanitate may function as a protective disclaimer

The Missing End of the Three Books

Sledge claims that the actual end of the Three Books of Occult Philosophy — a significant section — is missing from all modern editions including:

  • Purdue's edition (Eric Purdue, the translator)
  • Compagni's critical edition

To his knowledge, this ending has never been published in English. This means 99%+ of readers have never read the whole thing.

Magia Reformata: Why It Failed

Agrippa's program of magical reform failed to find followers. Sledge's explanation:

  1. Too heterodox for everyone: Too Catholic for Protestants, too magical for Counter-Reformation (Bruno was burned alive), too religious for Cartesians, too Christian for Enlightenment rationalists
  2. Too Christian for 19th-century occultists: Crowley and others wanted to move away from Christianity; Agrippa was inseparable from his Christian framework
  3. No practical results: The Three Books cannot be used to conjure spirits or get stuff done — useless for grimoire practitioners; necromancy (the popular form of magic) is incompatible with Agrippa's program
  4. Only Guillaume Postel took the magia reformata forward, and in a purely Kabbalistic direction

Mythology and Legend

Agrippa acquired a reputation for actually practicing black magic:

  • Black dog that accompanied him (Johann Weyer tried to debunk this)
  • Remote viewing — sent his spirit to spy on a wedding
  • Magical money — gold coins that turned into leaves
  • The unlucky boarder — a man read his magic book, devil killed him, Agrippa re-conjured the devil to animate the corpse and return it to town center
  • Goethe changed Faust's name from Johann to Heinrich in Faust to combine Agrippa with the historical Faust
  • Marlowe (Doctor Faustus) is one of the few who actually read Agrippa and combined his skepticism with magic in the play
  • Shelley has Frankenstein's father call Agrippa "sad trash" in Frankenstein
