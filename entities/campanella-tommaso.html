Tommaso Campanella

Overview

Tommaso Campanella (1568–1639) — Italian philosopher, poet, and political theorist — is described by Copenhaver as "the last great thinker to focus his genius on magic." A Dominican who ultimately proved unable to reconcile philosophy and theology, Campanella wrote extensively on natural magic, astrology, and what he called magia — a comprehensive system of cosmic and political reform. He spent 27 of his 71 years imprisoned for plotting against Spanish rule in Naples.

Copenhaver's treatment in Magic in Western Culture (2015) situates Campanella as the terminal figure of the learned magic tradition: after Campanella, the philosophical case for magic collapses under the combined weight of empiricism, philology, and the new mechanical philosophy.

Key Facts

  • Born: 1568, Stilo, Calabria
  • Died: 1639, Paris (after release from prison)
  • Education: Dominican novitiate at 14; studied philosophy and theology
  • Major works: La città del Sole (1602, published 1623); Magia et scientia; Atheismus Triumphatus (1631)
  • Imprisonment: 1599–1626 (27 years) for conspiracy against Spanish Habsburg rule; tortured; remained productive
  • Paris: Released through Urban VIII's intervention; died in Paris under Cardinal Richelieu's protection

Magical Philosophy

Campanella's magia was both philosophical and political. He sought a universal reformation of knowledge and society — a restauratio magicae that would restore the unity of knowledge disrupted since the Fall. His City of the Sun describes a utopian society governed by philosophical principles, with magic as the link between human knowledge and divine power.

Copenhaver emphasizes that Campanella's magic is continuous with Ficino's Neoplatonic tradition but pushed toward a more systematized cosmic holism:

  • The pneuma or spiritus as cosmic medium linking all things
  • Astrology as a genuine science of celestial influences
  • The magus as one who understands and manipulates these connections
  • A political dimension: magic as the basis for a reformed Christian society

Relationship to Other Figures

  • ficino-marsilio — Predecessor; Campanella continued and systematized Ficino's Neoplatonic magical philosophy
  • bruno-giordano — Contemporary; shared magical and mnemotechnical interests; both Italian magical philosophers
  • agrippa-heinrich-cornelius — Campanella borrowed from and built upon Agrippa's threefold magical system
  • pomponazzi-pietro — Campanella rejected Pomponazzi's naturalistic limiting of magic to explainable causes

Copenhaver's Verdict

"the last great thinker to focus his genius on magic"

Copenhaver treats Campanella as the culminating figure of the learned magic tradition: after Campanella, no major philosopher would defend magic as a systematic philosophical program. The Enlightenment's rejection of magic was not a single event but a gradual process, with Campanella representing the final, elaborate attempt to make magic philosophically respectable.
