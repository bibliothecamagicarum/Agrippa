Demonic Magic

Definition

Demonic magic — magic that works through the agency of demons or malevolent spirits — was a persistent and troubling dimension of Renaissance learned magic. The Renaissance magician who sought to manipulate supernatural beings faced a theological minefield: the Church condemned demonic summoning as heresy, yet the theoretical framework of Neoplatonic magic seemed to require it.

Copenhaver's central argument about the boundary between natural and demonic magic: Pagans and Christians had been testing this boundary for two millennia by Agrippa's time.

The Problem

The Neoplatonic cosmological framework that underpins Renaissance magic places spirits — angels and demons — at every level of the cosmos:

  • Celestial intelligences (angels) move the heavens
  • Aerial spirits inhabit the space between earth and moon
  • Demons occupy the sublunary region
  • All can be invoked, compelled, or bargained with

This makes demonic magic cosmologically plausible but theologically dangerous.

Natural Magic vs. Demonic Magic

The Renaissance debate centered on whether magic required demonic agency:

For demonic magic:

  • Ficino's De vita coelitus comparanda includes rituals to attract celestial spirits — some interpreted as demonic
  • Agrippa's third level of magic (ritual/ceremonial magic) invokes angelic or demonic intelligences
  • Popular magical texts (e.g., Picatrix) describe elaborate demonic summoning rituals

Against demonic magic:

  • Pomponazzi argued all apparently demonic effects have natural causes; demons unnecessary
  • Even Ficino insisted his spirit-invoking rituals were natural, not demonic
  • The Church demanded rejection of all demonic commerce; any suspected demonic connection could lead to Inquisition

Agrippa's Position

Copenhaver's Agrippa discusses the risk explicitly:

"Since angels and demons rule the upper stories of Agrippa's sympathetic cosmos, while stones, plants, and animals are in the basement but within reach of higher powers, the magus who taps the forces hidden in natural objects runs the risk of attracting supernatural attention, benevolent or malevolent."

Agrippa distinguishes three levels:

  1. Natural magic (natural causes only)
  2. Celestial magic (astrological influences)
  3. Ceremonial magic (invoking spirits) — the most dangerous

Witch-Hunts and Demonic Magic

The Renaissance obsession with demonic magic intersects with the witch-hunts: if demons could be invoked by magic, then anyone practicing magic risked the suspicion of demonic pact. Agrippa defended a woman accused of witchcraft — on legal grounds, not by denying the possibility of demonic magic.

Copenhaver: the line between learned magic and witchcraft was always contested, and the "disappearance" of the Renaissance magician into demonic rumor was partly the effect of the witch-hunt craze.

Key Figures

  • ficino-marsilio — His spirit-invoking rituals were sometimes read as demonic; he insisted they were natural
  • agrippa-heinrich-cornelius — Discussed demonic magic at length; distinguished it from natural; recanted magic in De Incertitudine
  • campanella-tommaso — Campanella's magic included angels; he was investigated by the Inquisition multiple times
  • pomponazzi-pietro — Eliminated demons from natural philosophy; made the natural/demonic boundary philosophically sharp

Related Concepts

  • spiritual-magic — Walker's term for the Ficinian tradition; spirits invoked but not demonic in the technical sense
  • demonic-pact — The theological category the Church used to condemn practitioners
  • witch-craze — The witch-hunts as context for the demonization of learned magic
