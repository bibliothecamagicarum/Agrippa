Pneuma and Spiritus

Definition

Pneuma (Greek) and spiritus (Latin) — a fine, rational, airy substance that ancient and Renaissance philosophers posited as the medium linking soul to body, earth to heaven, and human being to cosmic intelligence. The concept is the keystone of Renaissance magical philosophy: it is the explanatory mechanism that allows magic to work without demonic intervention.

Copenhaver/Walker: spiritus is "a physical and explanatory link between lightly embodied spiritual things (like the lower human soul) and highly rarefied material things (like smoke from a sacrifice)."

Ancient Origins

Stoicism

The Stoics posited pneuma as the animating principle of the cosmos — a rational breath that pervades all matter and constitutes the logos that orders the world. The Stoic cosmos is alive with rational spirit.

Neoplatonism

Plotinus and Proclus developed a more nuanced pneuma doctrine: it is the medium through which soul influences body, and through which celestial influences reach terrestrial things. The Neoplatonic pneuma is simultaneously:

  • Physical enough to transmit influences
  • Spiritual enough to receive and convey rational/celestial powers

The Chaldaean Oracles

A key source for Ficino: the Oracles describe pneuma as the cosmic medium through which theurgy (divine magic) operates. This theurgic pneuma tradition is what Ficino adapted for his magic.

The Music Analogy

Walker's key insight (Copenhaver's Chapter 1, pp. 22–24):

  • Air is the medium of music
  • Vibrating strings disturb the air, carrying the vibration to the listener
  • The planets and stars, moving in harmonic ratios, produce a cosmic music
  • The spiritus is the "air" of the cosmos: it carries celestial influences downward and human intentions upward

Ficino used this to explain how a magus could attract celestial powers through ritual practices: by manipulating the spiritus (through music, incense, images, or prayer), the magus attunes the cosmic medium to receive celestial influence.

In Renaissance Magic

Ficino

Ficino's De vita coelitus comparanda ("On Obtaining Life from the Heavens") describes rituals — including music, incense, and images — designed to attract celestial spiritus and thereby gain health, longevity, or magical power from the stars.

Agrippa

Agrippa's threefold magic (elemental/celestial/intellectual) operates through progressively subtler media:

  • Elemental world: occult qualities in material objects
  • Celestial world: figures, shapes, and astrological configurations — the spiritus carrying celestial influences
  • Intellectual world: ceremonies and invocations — drawing on intellectual/spiritual beings

The spiritus is present throughout, the medium of transmission between levels.

Pomponazzi

Pomponazzi used the pneuma concept to explain how natural objects could have effects disproportionate to their material constitution: the spiritus transmits the occult qualities of natural objects through the air, amplifying them.

Copenhaver's Historiographical Point

The pneuma/spiritus theory is what Copenhaver considers the correct explanation for why Renaissance magicians believed magic was philosophically defensible:

  • It provides a mechanism (not merely an authority)
  • It is grounded in ancient philosophical texts (Plotinus, Proclus, Chaldaean Oracles) — not the Hermetica
  • It explains how magic could be natural (not demonic) while still being genuinely effective

This is why Copenhaver sides with Walker over Yates: Walker's pneuma-based explanation is philosophically rigorous and historically accurate; Yates's "Hermetic tradition" misidentifies the source texts and mechanism.
