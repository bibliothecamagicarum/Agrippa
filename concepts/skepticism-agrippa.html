Agrippa's Skepticism

Definition

Agrippa's skepticism, articulated most fully in De incertitudine et vanitate scientiarum (1527; published 1530), is a radical philosophical position attacking the reliability of all human knowledge. Sledge calls it the "most controversial element in Agrippa's thinking" and the central interpretive puzzle of his entire oeuvre.

The De Incertitudine

A "diatribe" or declamation against the sciences — a classical rhetorical form that presents a radical position without necessarily requiring the author to fully endorse it. Agrippa goes through the sciences one by one, demonstrating their vanity and uncertainty.

Key features:

  • No human knowledge is certain; all is subject to doubt
  • Scholastic philosophy in particular is attacked as empty rationalism divorced from faith
  • The work draws on Academic and Pyrrhonian skepticism but ultimately slides toward fideism — the position that certainty belongs only to faith, not reason

The Tension with the Occult Philosophy

How can the same author write an enthusiastic Occult Philosophy celebrating magic and then publish a devastating attack on all knowledge claims?

Sledge's reading: the tension is real but resolvable:

  1. Esoteric reading: Agrippa is an esoteric writer who reveals truth only to the initiated. The De vanitate functions as a protective disclaimer — a public profession of skepticism that shields his deeper magical commitments.
  2. Fideism as completion: The De vanitate attacks reason, not faith. The skeptical turn moves magic from rational philosophy to revealed religion — the highest magic (theurgia) is grounded in fides, not ratio.
  3. Different audiences: The Occult Philosophy was written for initiates; the De vanitate for a broader, more skeptical public.

Influence on Descartes

Sledge claims — and this is a significant historical claim — that Descartes' skeptical method (the project of doubting everything to find something certain) was directly inspired by Descartes' reading of Agrippa's De Incertitudine.

This means Agrippa's skeptical turn influenced the very rationalism that would eventually displace him from the philosophical canon.

Relation to Hermeticism

Paradoxically, Agrippa's skepticism and his Hermeticism may be complementary rather than contradictory:

  • Hermeticism culminates in gnosis — direct divine illumination that transcends reason entirely
  • Skepticism clears away rational obstacles to receive that illumination
  • The De vanitate thus functions as a propaedeutic: it humbles the intellect so that faith (the ground of theurgy) can operate
