Spiritual Magic

Definition

"Spiritual magic" is Daniel Pickering Walker's term (adopted by Copenhaver) for the Ficinian tradition of magic that operates through the pneuma or spiritus — an intermediate substance that links the human soul to the cosmic order, enabling the magus to draw down celestial influences without invoking demons.

Copenhaver's formulation: "Magic that uses the spiritus as a medium... through which the strings of a lyre resonate with the cosmic music of planets and stars."

This is the key concept that distinguishes Ficinian magic from both:

  • Theurgical magic (which invokes divine beings directly)
  • Demonic magic (which compels or bargains with evil spirits)
  • Folk magic (which uses sympathetic sympathies without philosophical justification)

The Pneuma as Medium

The ancient theory, systematized by Walker:

  1. The cosmos is suffused with a fine, rational substance — pneuma (Greek) or spiritus (Latin)
  2. This substance is intermediate between mind and matter — "lightly embodied spiritual things" and "highly rarefied material things"
  3. Music demonstrates the principle: vibrating strings move the air, which carries the vibration to the listener
  4. The planets and stars produce a cosmic music through their motions
  5. The human soul, as rational spirit, can attune itself to these cosmic vibrations and draw power downward

Why "Spiritual"?

The term distinguishes this magic from:

  • Natural magic — operates through occult qualities in objects; no spiritual medium needed
  • Demonic magic — operates through invoked spirits; uses prayer/compulsion not attunement

Ficino's spiritual magic:

  • Is activated through ritual practices (music, prayer, incense, talismans)
  • But the ritual doesn't compel spirits — it tunes the spiritus, like tuning an instrument
  • The celestial influences flow through the attuned spiritus naturally, without demonic intervention

Walker's Discovery

Copenhaver credits Walker with identifying "the makings of the theory":

"The theory comes from a place and a time — Europe in the Renaissance — when Ficino and others were recovering philosophical doctrines (not Hermetic sermons) that Plotinus, Proclus, and other ancient sages had already used to explain and defend magic."

This is the core of Copenhaver's critique of Yates: the theoretical foundation of Renaissance magic is Neoplatonic (pneuma theory), not Hermetic (Corpus Hermeticum). The Hermetica are theological texts; the magic is philosophically grounded in Plotinus and Proclus.

Relationship to Other Concepts

  • pneuma-spiritus — The medium itself; central to spiritual magic
  • natural-magic — Natural magic is the result when spiritual magic works through natural objects
  • demonic-magic — Demonic magic uses compelled spirits; spiritual magic uses attunement
  • hermetism — Copenhaver argues that "Hermetic" magic is a misnomer; the correct term is "spiritual magic" grounded in Neoplatonism
  • yates-thesis — Directly contested; Yates's "Hermetic tradition" conflates the Hermetica (theological texts) with the Neoplatonic magical tradition (philosophical texts)
