The Elizabethan Age and the Occult Philosophy

Definition

Yates argues that the "Elizabethan Age" — the cultural flowering of late 16th-century England — was fundamentally shaped by the "occult philosophy" (Hermeticism + Christian Cabala). The major literary and intellectual figures of the period: Spenser, Shakespeare, Marlowe, John Dee, and the Sidney circle, are all read through this lens.

Core Argument

The book moves in two parts:

  1. Part 1: Abbreviated history of the occult philosophy — Lull → Pico → Reuchlin → Giorgi → Agrippa → Dürer → Witch-craze reaction
  2. Part 2: The Elizabethan manifestations — Dee (Christian Cabalist, magus to Elizabeth I), Spenser's Faerie Queene (Neoplatonic/Hermetic poem of Protestant reform), Marlowe's Doctor Faustus (reflecting witch-hunts against Agrippa), Chapman's Shadow of Night (Düreresque inspired melancholy), Milton, and the Return of the Jews

The implicit question: Why did this remarkable cultural flourishing — centered on figures like Dee, Sidney, Spenser — end so completely, leaving only "clouds of demonic rumour"?

Key Theses

John Dee: The philosopher of the Elizabethan age. Dee "could base himself on Agrippa's occult angelology whilst at the same time believing himself to be the ardent supporter of a widespread Christian reform." He was the Christian Cabalist, using Hermetic-Cabalist techniques for Protestant ends.

Spenser's Faerie Queene: Read as Hermetic-Cabalist Neoplatonism "adapted to the expression of a northern poetic reformation." The poem's fairy world is the occult philosophy universe made epic.

Marlowe's Doctor Faustus: Yates's most controversial reading — she reads Faustus as "the reflection of the contemporary witch-hunts against Agrippa." The play uses Agrippa's name as a signal of black magic, reflecting the period's conflation of white and black magic that destroyed the magicians' reputations.

The Sidney circle: Philip Sidney, Edward Dyer, and others formed the inner group learning from Dee. Spenser was connected through this network.

The witch-craze as reaction: The "reactions against the occult philosophy" (Chapter VII of Part 1) represent the broader cultural turn against Renaissance magic that eventually consumed Dee and transformed Agrippa into the black magician of legend.

Puritan occultism: The Elizabethan movement had "strong Puritan elements" — the occult philosophy was "a Puritan version" of Renaissance Hermetism, adapted to Protestant reform. Leicester, Sidney, and Spenser were all "Puritan" in this sense.

Yates's Historiographical Position

Yates is explicit: "This book must begin with the statement that, like all my other books, it is a strictly historical study. It is not an enquiry into 'the occult' in general." Rather, it is about "what was known as 'the occult philosophy' in the Renaissance."

She defines "the occult philosophy" as: "compounded of Hermeticism as revived by Marsilio Ficino, to which Pico della Mirandola added a Christianised version of Jewish Cabala."

Critiques

Hanegraaff's critique applies here: Yates's "optical illusion" means her reading of Elizabethan culture as Hermetic is itself based on the same systematic misreading of sources — attributing to Hermetism what actually derives from the Asclepius and Lazzarellian sources unknown to the Elizabethan figures.
