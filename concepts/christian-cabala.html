Christian Cabala

Definition

Christian Cabala is the use of Jewish Kabbalistic techniques — particularly the manipulation of Hebrew letters, the Tetragrammaton, and the Sephiroth — in the service of Christian theology and religious reform. Founded by Pico della Mirandola in the 1480s, it became a central strand of Renaissance occult philosophy, combining with Hermeticism to form the "occult philosophy" that Yates argues runs through the entire Renaissance and Reformation.

Foundational Figures

Pico della Mirandola (1463–1494): Founded Christian Cabala. He learned Hebrew from Jewish teachers and interpreted Kabbalah in a Christian direction — Cabala confirms Christianity, particularly the divinity of Christ. His Cabalist Conclusions: the Name of Jesus contains all the power of the Tetragrammaton. Pico's famous claim: "Nulla est scientia quae nos magis certificet de divinitate Christi quam Magia et Cabala" — no science confirms Christ's divinity like magic and Cabala.

Johannes Reuchlin (1455–1522): The German pioneer. De Verbo Mirifico (1494) and De Arte Cabbalistica (1517). Reuchlin's Cabala is less radical than Pico's but still a "more powerful philosophy" — Kabbalistic technique applied to Christian ends. He was the direct influence on Agrippa.

Francesco Giorgi (Zorzi) (c. 1460–1540): The Cabalist Friar of Venice. His De harmonia mundi (1525) is the quintessence of early 16th-century Platonism combined with Christian Cabala, Hermetic elements, and Franciscan mysticism. A censored and influential text — Yates calls it "the quintessence of the early sixteenth-century Platonism."

Cardinal Egidius of Viterbo: Leader of Catholic reform through Christian Cabala. Collected Cabalist manuscripts. "The discovery of the Cabala by the Christians of his own day marked a great turning-point which affected all humanity."

Agrippa's Version

Agrippa's De occulta philosophia "can be classed as Christian Cabala" — it leads up, in Book III, to the Name of Jesus as all-powerful, containing the power of the Tetragrammaton. Agrippa follows Pico's Cabalist Conclusions in this.

Yates: "Christian Cabala is leading to a kind of evangelicalism, supported by the occult philosophy." Agrippa combines Erasmian evangelical reform with the "more powerful philosophy" of ceremonial magic.

Function of Christian Cabala

  1. Proof of Christian doctrine: The Tetragrammaton, manipulated through Cabalist letter-mysticism, confirms Christ's divinity
  2. Magical power: Hebrew letters and names have inherent power — not just symbolic meaning
  3. Protection against demons: Cabala is "insurance against demons" — it guarantees the operator draws only good angelic influences
  4. Religious reform: Both Catholic reformers (Egidius, Giorgi) and Protestant-leaning humanists (Reuchlin, Agrippa) used Christian Cabala for reform

Tensions

Erasmus vs. Agrippa on ceremonial magic: Erasmus became suspicious of "Judaising" — the attempt to increase magic in Christian ceremonies. This is precisely what Agrippa's De occulta philosophia Book III does. Yates sees a "diametric opposition" between Erasmian reform (Gospel simplicity) and Agrippan reform (magical power).

Censorship and the Counter-Reformation: Giorgi's works became impossible to defend. The censors found heresies in the "cultural latitudinarianism, Erasmian tendency, and Platonic, Hermetic, and Cabalist elements." The Council of Trent hardened the lines — Catholic orthodoxy would be Aristotelian, not Hermetic-Cabalist.

In Elizabethan England

Yates traces Christian Cabala to England through John Dee. Dee "could base himself on Agrippa's occult angelology whilst at the same time believing himself to be the ardent supporter of a widespread Christian reform." This is the English manifestation of the same Hermetic-Cabalist tradition.

The "Puritan" variant: "Puritan Christian Cabalists" — Leicester, Sidney, Spenser — used Hermetic-Cabalist techniques for Protestant imperial reform.
