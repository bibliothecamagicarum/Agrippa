Key Arguments

  1. Magic as Classical Philosophical Tradition — Magic in the West is not primitive religion or proto-science but a learned philosophical tradition with roots in Neoplatonism, Iamblichean theurgy, and Peripatetic physics (occult qualities). The tradition runs from Plotinus through Proclus, Ficino, Pomponazzi, Agrippa, Bruno, Campanella, Fludd, and into the Enlightenment.

  2. Against the Yates Thesis — Copenhaver directly critiques Frances Yates's "Hermetic tradition" reading of Renaissance magic. The Hermetica (Corpus Hermeticum) are not about magic; Ficino's magic derives from Plotinus, Proclus, and the Chaldaean Oracles — not from the Hermetic sermons. Yates was right to focus on magic but wrong to call it "Hermetic." Students of tradition might worry that Yates misuses that term. (p. 22)

  3. Walker over Yates — Daniel Pickering Walker's Spiritual and Demonic Magic from Ficino to Campanella (1958) provides the correct model: the theoretical foundation for learned magic is the Neoplatonic doctrine of pneuma/spiritus as a medium linking soul, cosmos, and spirit — grounded in music theory, not Hermetic texts. Copenhaver credits Walker as the more careful scholar whose work Yates's popularity eclipsed.

  4. Pomponazzi as Naturalist Magician — Pomponazzi's De incantationibus (1520, printed 1556) argues that "marvelous" phenomena have natural causes explainable by Aristotelian physics, including occult qualities. His naturalism is misread by those who see his final capitulation to ecclesiastical authority as insincere; Copenhaver reads it as genuine: philosophy and faith operate in separate domains. Pomponazzi is both a conservative Aristotelian and an advocate of natural magic.

  5. Agrippa as Compiler, not Originator — Agrippa "copied, digested and reorganized" the arguments of Ficino, Pico, Pomponazzi, and others. His De occulta philosophia is a vulgarization, not an original work. Its success came from being a compendium — "not only natural, spiritual, and demonic magic, but also alchemy, astrology, Kabbalah, divination, ritual, theurgy, and witchcraft."

  6. Campanella as Last Magician — Tommaso Campanella (1568–1639) is the "last great thinker to focus his genius on magic." His magia is both philosophical and political, aiming at universal reform. Imprisoned for 27 years for conspiracy against Spanish rule in Naples.

  7. Bookish Magic and the Pictures — New pictorial technologies (perspective, woodcut, engraving) enhanced the credibility of magical objects by textualizing them. Agrippa's dragon, the basilisk, the magnet — these become "hypertextualized," more real throughbooks than through observation. The new art made the old magic more credible before empirical philosophy dismantled both.

  8. Frazer's Problem — The book opens with J.G. Frazer's Golden Bough as a misframing of magic that still haunts the field. Frazer's "primitive" magic bears little relation to the learned philosophical magic of the Renaissance.

  9. The Enlightenment Repudiation — Magic was repudiated not by a single breakthrough (Bacon/Cartesian rationalism) but through a gradual accumulation of philological, empirical, and philosophical critiques. The process begins before Descartes and ends after Newton.

  10. Against "Occultism" — Copenhaver avoids the term occultism until after Agrippa, preferring magic as the more historically precise term for the learned tradition.

Historiographical Position

Copenhaver occupies a distinct position in the field:

  • Against Yates: The Hermetica are not magical texts; the "Hermetic tradition" misnames what is actually a Neoplatonic-theurgical tradition rooted in Plotinus, Proclus, and the Chaldaean Oracles.
  • With Walker: Walker's pneuma/spiritus model is the correct explanatory framework for how Renaissance magicians understood their art.
  • With Hanegraaff (partial): Both Copenhaver and Hanegraaff agree that Yates over-emphasized the Hermetica. Copenhaver differs in focusing on natural magic and philosophical foundations rather than Lazzarellian gnosis and divine generation.
  • Against Thorndike: Lynn Thorndike's "magic as progress toward science" reading is rejected; magic had philosophical foundations that were not proto-scientific.
