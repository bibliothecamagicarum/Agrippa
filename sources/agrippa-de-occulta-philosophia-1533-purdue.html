De Occulta Philosophia Libri Tres (1533) — Purdue Translation

Source Notes

Provenance

  • Type: Primary source — Agrippa's own text
  • Edition: Eric Purdue translation (modern English); published by Three Hands Press
  • Original publication: Cologne, 1533
  • Ingest: 2026-04-27 from local corpus file

Structure: The Three Books

Book One — The Natural World (Chs. 1–74):

  • Elements, qualities, natural virtues
  • Stones, metals, plants, animals
  • The mind, passions, speech, writing, divination

Book Two — The Celestial World (Chs. 1–64):

  • Celestial virtues flowing from stars and planets
  • Images and sigils of planets, signs, mansions, fixed stars
  • Geomancy, magical elections, characters, World Soul, celestial spirits
  • Human connection to higher spirits

Book Three — The Intellectual World / Divine Magic (Chs. 1–65):

  • The three orders of spirits (gods, intelligences, souls)
  • Human soul, divine and superior forces
  • Necromancy, prophecy, ecstasy, frenzy
  • Purity, purification, consecration, sacred things, sacred names, sacred times
  • Conclusion (Ch. 65)

Key Arguments in the Text

The threefold world: There is a threefold world — elemental, celestial, and intellectual. Each inferior is ruled by its superior, accepting virtues flowing from the Archetype through angels, heavens, stars, elements, animals, plants, metals, and stones. Magic operates across all three.

Magic defined: "Magic is the most perfect and highest science, the highest and most sacred philosophy. Finally, it is the absolute consummation of the noblest philosophy." It unifies natural science, mathematics, and theology.

The threefold magic: Magic requires all three faculties — natural (physical), mathematical (celestial), and theological (divine). No perfect magical work exists without all three.

The elemental world: The four elements exist everywhere — in the inferiors they are feculent and dense, in the heavens pure and clear, in the supercelestials truly living and blessed.

Idea and seminal reason: Individuals born under determined horoscopes receive extraordinary virtues of labor and suffering. Stones, plants, and animals have occult properties determined by celestial configuration at conception.

Theurgic ascent: Wise men can rise through each world, through each degree, toward the archetypal world — the Maker of all and the First Cause.

Purity and religion: The practitioner must be clean inside and out, abstain from foulness and desire. God receives what is offered by a purified and well-disposed man. "Prayer is good with fasting and alms."

Dispersed intentions: Agrippa explicitly says he has written the work so that "what is hidden in one place, we will make plain in another." He deliberately dispersed his intentions across the text. This matches Perrone Compagni's concept of dispersa intentio.

Sacred times: Magicians must observe sacred and religious days no less than planetary days. Those days are more effective for acquiring spiritual and divine virtues because those virtues descend from intelligences and the supercelestial world, not from elements or celestial bodies.

Names and their power: Wonderful powers reside in names — Hebrew, Egyptian, Greek, Latin. "Favorable names return more fortunate things to us; however, unfavorable names return unfortunate things."

The conclusion's esotericism: Chapter 65 addresses "sons of learning and wisdom" vs. "spiteful slanderers, sons of crooked ignorance." The secrets are hidden from the crooked, delivered to the prudent and intelligent. This is explicit esoteric framing.

Key Entities in the Text

  • agrippa-heinrich-cornelius — author
  • ficino-marsilio — major source (Plato, Pimander, natural magic)
  • pico-della-mirandola-giovanni — cited as source
  • reuchlin-johannes — De Verbo Mirifico and De Arte Cabbalistica; Kabbalistic material

Key Concepts in the Text

  • magic-learned — the threefold magic defined as the highest science
  • microcosm-man — human contains all levels of reality; can ascend through each world
  • hermetic-deification — rising through degrees to the archetypal world
  • restauratio-magicae — Agrippa states his purpose: "I would restore the reason that adorns magic itself"
  • alchemy-agrippa — metals, transmutation, the philosopher's stone mentioned
  • prisca-theologia — ancient wisdom from Egyptians, Chaldeans, Hebrew kabbalists, Orphics, Pythagoreans, Platonists, Brahmans, Gymnosophists
  • dispersa-intentio — explicitly stated in the text: secrets dispersed across the work
  • gnosis — illumination, prophetic dreams, direct divine knowledge
  • spiritual-regeneration — purity, purification, consecration as path to divine virtue
  • divine-generation — the soul as offspring and image of God; receiving divine virtue

Sledge's Claim About the Missing End

Sledge claims the actual end of the Three Books — a significant section beyond Chapter 65 — is missing from all modern editions including Purdue's. The text in this file ends at "FINISHED IN THE YEAR 1533, THE MONTH OF JULY" (Chapter 65). Whether there is additional text that was suppressed or lost is not verifiable from this edition.

Note on Sources

Purdue's translation includes extensive source citations. Major sources used by Agrippa include:

  • Ficino's complete works
  • Pliny the Elder
  • Picatrix (Ghāyat al-Ḥakīm)
  • Ptolemy's Tetrabiblos
  • Orphic hymns
  • Orapollo's Hieroglyphica
  • Albertus Magnus
  • William of Paris
  • Roger Bacon
