About the Book

Lehrich's study (a revised dissertation, supervised by Allen G. Debus, Jonathan Z. Smith, and Anthony C. Yu) is the most sustained close-reading of DOP in modern scholarship. Where Thorndike dismissed DOP as a "disappointing" compendium and Yates treated Agrippa as a stepping stone to Bruno and Dee, Lehrich argues that DOP is a philosophically coherent, sophisticated work with a genuine argumentative arc — and that its "secret key" lies in its theory of written signs.

Key Arguments

  1. DOP is not a compilation but a coherent philosophical argument. The standard scholarly view (Thorndike, Nauert) that DOP is a mere anthology of other writers' ideas, loosely organized, is wrong. Agrippa's text has genuine logical structure and philosophical focus.

  2. Magic is fundamentally a linguistic/semiotic project. Agrippa's magic works through signs — spoken words (voces), written characters (characteres), sigils, and inscribed figures. The power of the sign to lock onto its referent is the mechanism of magical efficacy.

  3. The "secret key" is the text itself. Agrippa's famous claim of a secret key to DOP, accessible only to "wise" readers, refers to the semiotic theory embedded in the text — a theory that required the full three books to develop.

  4. Agrippa's ritual magic is writing-centered, not speech-centered. This is Lehrich's most original theoretical contribution. Standard ritual theory (derived from Frazer, Malinowski, Tambiah) privileges speech over writing as the paradigmatic ritual act. Agrippa's DOP inverts this: the highest magic works through inscription, characters, and images — a "writing" theory of ritual.

  5. Demonic magic is integral, not embarrassing. Book III's ceremonial/demonic magic is not an embarrassment Agrippa should be excused from — it is the culmination of the philosophical project begun in Books I and II.

  6. The De vanitate/DOP tension is resolvable. The apparent contradiction between Agrippa's skeptical De vanitate (1526) and his DOP (1531/33) resolves once one recognizes that DOP is a philosophical exercise, demonstrating what magic would be if coherent — a demonstration that ultimately undercuts itself.

Methodology

Lehrich applies three disciplinary lenses:

  • Anthropology/History of Religions: Engages Frazer's "proto-science" definition of magic, Malinowski's "magic, science, religion" trichotomy, and the "illicit religion" and "social cleavage" models
  • History of Ideas: Traces Agrippa's influence on Bruno, Dee, Paracelsus, Descartes
  • Textual Criticism/Semiotics: Close reading of DOP's Latin; theory of analog signification; writing-centered ritual theory

Position in Historiography

  • Against Thorndike: DOP is not a mere bibliography of magic
  • Against Yates: Agrippa is not just a precursor to Bruno and Dee — he is a philosophical thinker in his own right with a systematic project
  • Complementary to Copenhaver: Copenhaver focuses on magic as classical philosophical tradition (pneuma, natural magic); Lehrich focuses on magic as semiotic-linguistic practice (signs, sigils, writing). Both reject Yates's Hermetic framework but emphasize different aspects.
